What to do in the garden in April: 10 early-spring jobs

Publish date: 2024-06-04

A guide on what to do in the garden in April, including deadheading daffodils, keeping on top of weeds, maintaining spring borders and preparing hanging baskets.

With the hum of busy bees and the whistling of birds through the trees, April brings us moments to savour. But don’t be fooled by the sunny days and rising temperatures, as overnight weather can still lead to a damaging frost.

So, with plant frost protection close at hand, here are 10 jobs you can be doing right now that’ll help your garden thrive.

Maintenance

1. Deadhead

Daffodil displays will now be starting to wane, so deadhead them before they go to seed and allow the foliage to dieback naturally. This will transfer the plant’s energy back into the bulb, helping it to produce a new, healthy plant next spring.

2. Weeds

As plants grow, so will weeds start to appear. Therefore, hoe regularly to prevent them smothering young plants and seedlings. Weeds can also hide pests, such as slugs and snails, that will happily devour your prized blooms. Once removed, mulch at the base of the plants to suppress further weeds and help retain moisture.

3. Pinch out

Greenhouse grown plants, such as sweet peas, will be putting on growth. To create a bushy plant, that will produce more blooms, pinch out the growing tips. The plant will develop more side-shoots, giving you a healthier plant. By the end of the month sweet peas can be planted out into their final growing positions. Ensure they have a support their tendrils can latch onto. As they grow tall, they will also need tying-in.

4. Harden off

Towards the end of April, you should start to harden off certain plants in a cold frame. This is simply getting plants that were sown indoors acclimatised to cooler, outdoor temperatures. A few weeks growing on in a cold frame will ensure they develop into strong plants. If you don’t have a cold frame, place your plants outside for a few hours every day. Do this for a couple of weeks before planting. Remember to bring them in before the temperature drops, or if the weather takes a turn for the worse.

5. Spring bedding

Pansies and violas will be keen to set seed, remove faded flowers regularly to encourage new displays. With primroses, it’s an ideal time to increase your stock for next season by lifting and dividing. Whether it’s with your hands, or a trowel, prise the plants apart. Don’t worry about damaging them as they’re quite tough. Re-plant where you would like to see them appear next year.

Seeds

6. Sow

Parsnips, carrots and salads can now be sown. If the ground is still too cold, sow seeds into trays and pots. Keep the soil moist, and place pots somewhere warm and bright. For outdoor growing, two weeks before sowing, cover over the area with a cloche or horticultural fleece to warm the soil. With seeds sown, replace the structures to help protect emerging seedlings.

7. Thinning out

If you sowed seeds back in March, seedlings should now be appearing. By thinning them out, remaining seedlings can grow on unchallenged to become healthy plants. Simply hold the seedling by its 'true' leaves, not its stem. If a leaf gets damaged it can produce another. However, if you damage the stem the plant cannot recover. Seedlings may also be at a stage where they can be pricked out and individually potted on into larger pots to grow on.

8. Wildflowers

Wildflowers are a great way to encourage wildlife to your garden. If you have a sunny, bare patch, remove all weeds and stones, dig over and consolidate the soil to create a fine tilth. Wildflowers can be bought in pre-mixed packets, giving you a large variety of different flower types. Scatter the seeds finely over the growing area, lightly rake and water gently.

Containers

9. Hanging baskets

Hanging baskets are an easy way to introduce colour to a sunny patio. From trailing petunias to blousy begonias, garden nurseries are now filled with a wide range of plants to choose from. Plant up baskets using good multi-purpose compost and a slow-release fertiliser. Add water retention granules to the bottom of the basket, as it’ll help keep roots moist during the hottest of summer days. Once potted up, place in the greenhouse until the last frost has passed, giving plants a chance to become established. Then, harden off and place them in their final hanging positions.

Herbs

10. Growing

If you don’t have space for a herb garden, try growing in pots and containers. Give plants plenty of sunshine, and plant them into well-gritted soil to ensure good drainage. Herbs often originate from hot climates, so try to replicate these conditions, and don’t overwater. Also, prune established lavender, sage and rosemary. Remove any dead and diseased foliage but avoid cutting into the woody parts of the plant.
