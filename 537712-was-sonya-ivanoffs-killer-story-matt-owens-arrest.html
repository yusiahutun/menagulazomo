Was Sonya Ivanoff's killer found? Her story and cop Matt Owens' arrest

Publish date: 2024-08-14

In August 2003, the brutal murder of Sonya Ivanoff sent shockwaves through the small coastal city of Nome, Alaska, United States. Two days after her disappearance, Sonya's lifeless body was found inside a gravel pit hidden in a bush on the outskirts of Nome. The incident sparked conflicting feelings among those who followed the saddening story, with many seeking to know whether Sonya Ivanoff's killer was found.

Who is Sonya Ivanoff?

Sonya Dora Ivanoff was born on 13 April 1984 in Unalakleet, Nome Census Area, Alaska, United States, to Larry M. Ivanoff and Maggie. She grew up alongside her five siblings, including Jacob and Christina Mostoller. However, the identity of her other siblings is unclear.

Dora was a basketball player while in high school before graduating in 2002. Later, the Unalakleet native moved to Nome, where she worked at the admitting desk of Norton Sound Regional Hospital. She was also a beauty queen who participated in pageants for native women.

What happened to Sonya Ivanoff?

At around 1 am on 11 August 2003, Sonya decided to walk home alone to her apartment after enjoying a night out with friends. Unfortunately, that was the last time her friends saw her alive. Several hours later, after her disappearance, her roommate, Timayre Towarak, called the police to report her missing after she failed to return home.

A search and rescue team immediately swung into action. On the same day, at 8:30 pm, Sonya's remains were discovered inside a gravel pit hidden in a bush on the outskirts of Nome. Ivanoff's lifeless body was naked, with only a sock on the left foot, and she had a gunshot wound at the back of her head. She had bruises on her face, neck, and chest.

Sonya's murder case was immediately handed over to the Alaska State Troopers. An autopsy report later revealed that the basketball player died as a result of a gunshot wound at the back of her skull, fired from close range.

Who was Sonya Ivanoff's killer?

The investigating officers first implicated Sonya's crush, Andrew Donnelly. Andrew cooperated with investigators and was cleared of the charges. Four weeks later, after Ivanoff was murdered, two sisters, Florence Habros and Dannite Malywotkuk, called the police and revealed that they saw Sonya walking alone in the early morning hours of 11 August 2003.

Dannite knew Sonya after she saw her play in a city basketball game. The two sisters told the police that at about 1:26 am, Nome's police vehicle picked her up as she walked down the street. Investigations later discovered that the police car was in the hands of Alaska police officer Matt Owens at the time Sonya was picked up.

Later, Mathew and his colleague Stan Piscoya who were both on duty at Nome Police Department during the night of Sonya's disappearance took a polygraph exam. Stan passed the test, while Owens failed the test. Consequently, Owens was arrested on 25 October 2003 and charged with the murder of Sonya Ivanoff.

After his arrest, several women came forward and accused him of sexually harassing them while on duty and threatening them with arrest if they ever spoke up about it.

The ex-police officer was indicted for first-degree murder on 4 November 2003. Even though his first trial in 2005 ended in a hung jury, Owens was eventually convicted of the murder of Sonya Ivanoff and tampering with evidence on 6 December 2005. He was sentenced to 101 years in prison.

In September 2015, Mathew filed a petition in court seeking post-conviction relief, claiming ineffective assistance from his counsel during his second trial held in Kotzebue. However, his attempt to secure his freedom from a 101-year sentence failed.

Nome, Alaska disappearances

Nome, Alaska, had a population of 3,699, as recorded in the 2020 census. The small community of Nome is known for the mysterious disappearance of 24 people that happened between the 1960s and 2004. All these occurred in Nome and surrounding villages.

Through FBI investigation, it was determined that these disappearances were largely a combination of excessive alcohol consumption and a harsh winter climate.
